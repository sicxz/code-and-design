The depth illusion in action

The perceptual science

Your brain already
knows this.

Look out the window of a moving car. Nearby telephone poles fly past. Mountains on the horizon barely seem to move at all. Your brain uses this difference in speed — called motion parallax — to judge distance.

Web parallax borrows that exact heuristic. Background layers move slowly; foreground layers move fast. The brain reads the speed difference as spatial separation — depth — even on a completely flat screen.

This is a monocular depth cue: it works with one eye, no stereoscopic vision required. It's hardwired, not learned.

The perceptual chain.

Closer objects move faster across the retina than distant ones.

This is the rule the physical world follows. Web parallax exploits it: if a background image scrolls at 0.5× the speed of the page, your visual system infers it must be farther away.

Motion parallax is one of the strongest monocular depth cues in perception science. It's why the effect feels convincing even when you know the screen is flat.

animation state.

This is the one concept that explains all scroll-driven animation. It's not a timer controlling the animation — it's the user's scroll position. Distance replaces time.

Three related terms, clearly separated.

Parallax
Visual effect where layers move at different speeds. Creates depth. Not inherently narrative.
Scrollytelling
The story unfolds as you scroll. Narrative is the goal; scroll-driven animation is the tool.
Scroll-Driven Animation
The technical mechanism behind both. Animation progress = scroll progress. Not time-based.
Key distinction

Parallax creates spatial depth. Scrollytelling creates narrative progression. Scroll-driven animation is what makes both possible technically. You can have parallax without scrollytelling, and scrollytelling without parallax.

The designer's read

Scrollytelling works best when the story has a clear beginning, middle, and end — a linear sequence. If users need to compare or scan non-linearly, scrollytelling is the wrong tool.

History

From plugin-dependent
to native browser.

The technology changed. The goal — immersive, scroll-driven narrative — stayed constant. Here's the arc.

2000s

The Flash Era

Adobe Flash enabled timeline-based animations inside a browser plugin. Designers created elaborate, scroll-reactive experiences — but they required the plugin, were inaccessible to screen readers, and died on mobile.

Plugin-based
2012

New York Times — "Snow Fall"

A long-form avalanche story combining scroll-triggered video, parallax backgrounds, and animated diagrams. It wasn't the first to use scroll effects, but its quality and reach made scrollytelling a named genre. It proved the technique could serve serious journalism.

Landmark moment
2015+

Apple's Product Pages

Apple perfected the commercial scrollytelling pattern: pin a section, reveal features one-by-one as the user scrolls. This created a controlled, cinematic pacing that became an industry template.

Commercial benchmark
2017

IntersectionObserver API

Browsers shipped a native way to detect when elements enter the viewport without expensive scroll event listeners. Better than polling, but it didn't handle animation sync to scroll position — only entry/exit detection.

Browser API
2018+

GSAP ScrollTrigger

GreenSock shipped ScrollTrigger, which became the industry standard for complex scroll animation. Pin, scrub, timeline sequencing — all tied to scroll position with cross-browser reliability. Free since Webflow acquired GSAP in 2024.

Industry standard
2023–

Native CSS Scroll-Driven Animations

The W3C spec stabilized and browsers shipped animation-timeline. Simple scroll effects now require zero JavaScript and run on the compositor thread — not the main thread. The future is declarative.

Now

Case study

"Snow Fall" — why it
changed everything.

NYT · December 2012

Snow Fall: The Avalanche at Tunnel Creek

John Branch's story about a deadly Cascade avalanche used custom JavaScript to synchronize photography, video, and animated diagrams with the reader's scroll. It wasn't technically novel — but the execution was so polished it felt different in kind, not just degree. The web press called it a watershed moment.

What made it work: the animations served the story. The parallax wasn't decoration — it placed the reader in terrain. The scroll-locked sections focused attention. The motion had meaning.

What it usedCustom JavaScript scroll event listeners — the hard, expensive way
Why it matteredHigh-profile proof that scroll-driven design could serve editorial journalism
What it namedThe term "scrollytelling" entered common usage after this piece
The lessonMotion with narrative purpose > motion as decoration. Always.

Modern scrollytelling

The Pudding model.

The Pudding (pudding.cool) is the best working example of scroll-driven data journalism. They make complex information comprehensible by breaking it into a step-by-step scroll sequence.

Each scroll step reveals one piece of data, one comparison, one insight. The reader controls the pace. The scroll becomes a tool for comprehension — not a design flourish.

The pattern they use

Pin a data visualization on one side. Let explanatory text scroll past it on the other. As each text block enters view, the visualization updates. The static chart becomes a story told chapter by chapter.

The failure mode to avoid

Scrollytelling where the animation is the point, not the content. If removing the animation would break comprehension, you've used it well. If it's still readable without it, the animation was just decoration.
